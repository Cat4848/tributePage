James Watt

A man who changed the course of history

James Watt's life may be summarised as follows:

  • 1736 – born on 19 January in Scotland.
  • 1753 – went to Glasgow to study mathematics and instruments making.
  • 1755 – goes to London to study. This is where he found a master to train him.
  • 1757 – returns to Glasgow: he opened a shop at the university and made mathematical instruments (e.g., quadrants, compasses, scales).
  • 1764 – he married his cousin Margaret Miller, who, before she died nine years later, bore him six children.
  • 1765 – invents the separate condenser, his first and greatest invention. Watt had realized that the loss of latent heat (the heat involved in changing the state of a substance—e.g., solid or liquid) was the worst defect of the Newcomen engine and that therefore condensation must be effected in a chamber distinct from the cylinder but connected to it.
  • 1769 – takes out the famous patent for “A New Invented Method of Lessening the Consumption of Steam and Fuel in Fire Engines.”
  • 1766 – becomes a land surveyor.
  • 1774 – moves to Birmingham, England
  • 1775 – begins a 25 years-long business partnership with Matthew Boulton.
  • 1776 – two engines were installed: one for pumping water in a Staffordshire colliery, the other for blowing air into the furnaces of British industrialist John Wilkinson.
  • 1776 – marries again; his second wife, Ann MacGregor, bore him two more children.
  • 1782 – patentes the double-acting engine, in which the piston pushed as well as pulled.
  • 1784 – invents the parallel motion: an arrangement of connected rods that guided the piston rod in a perpendicular motion.
  • 1785 – elected fellow of the Royal Society of London.
  • 1790 – invents the pressure gauge, which virtually completes the Watt engine.
  • 1795 – gradually withdraws from business.
  • 1806 – he was made doctor of laws of the University of Glasgow.
  • 1814 – becomes foreign associate of the French Academy of Sciences.
  • 1819 – dies on 25 August (aged 83) in Birmingham, England.
